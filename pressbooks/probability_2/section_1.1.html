1.1. What is probability?

At its core, probability describes random events—also called stochastic. The word comes from the Greek στοχαστικός, meaning “to aim” or “to guess.” We assign to each event a number \( \mathbb{P}(A) \in [0,1] \) expressing uncertainty or “surprise.”

  • If an event \(A\) never occurs, \( \mathbb{P}(A)=0 \).
  • If it always occurs, \( \mathbb{P}(A)=1 \).
  • Otherwise, \( \mathbb{P}(A)\in (0,1) \).

For a fair coin, the sample space is \( \Omega = \{H,T\} \). Symmetry gives:

\[ \begin{equation} \mathbb{P}(H) = \mathbb{P}(T) = \frac12 \end{equation} \]

The frequentist definition interprets probability as the limit of relative frequency:

\[ \begin{equation} \mathbb{P}(A) = \lim_{N \to \infty} \frac{n(A)}{N} \end{equation} \]

Probability must satisfy unitarity:

\[ \begin{equation} \sum_{i=1}^{n} \mathbb{P}(x_i) = 1 \end{equation} \]

For a fair die, this becomes:

\[ \begin{equation} \frac{1}{6} + \frac{1}{6} + \cdots + \frac{1}{6} = 1 \end{equation} \]

A probability space is the triple \( (\Omega,\mathcal{F},\mathbb{P}) \): outcomes, measurable events, and a probability measure. Kolmogorov formalized probability with three axioms:

  • Non-negativity: \( \mathbb{P}(A)\ge 0 \).
  • Normalization: \( \mathbb{P}(\Omega)=1 \).
  • Countable additivity:
\[ \begin{equation} \mathbb{P}\!\left(\bigcup_{i=1}^\infty A_i\right) = \sum_{i=1}^\infty \mathbb{P}(A_i), \qquad A_i\ \text{disjoint} \end{equation} \]

Two major interpretations coexist: the frequentist view (long-run frequency) and the Bayesian view (degree of belief). Both rest on the modern measure-theoretic framework.
