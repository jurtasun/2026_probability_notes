1.2. Discrete random variables

A random variable assigns numerical values to outcomes of a random process. When these values form a countable set, the variable is called discrete. Examples include coin tosses, dice rolls, and event counts.

Bernoulli events

A variable \(X\) is Bernoulli with parameter \(p\) if:

\[ \begin{equation} \mathbb{P}(X=1)=p, \qquad \mathbb{P}(X=0)=1-p \end{equation} \]

Binomial distribution

The Binomial distribution counts successes in \(n\) independent Bernoulli trials:

\[ \begin{equation} \mathbb{P}(X=k)=\binom{n}{k}\,p^k(1-p)^{n-k} \end{equation} \]

Cumulative probability:

\[ \begin{equation} \mathbb{P}(X\le 2)=\sum_{k=0}^{2}\binom{10}{k}(0.3)^k(0.7)^{10-k} \end{equation} \]

Poisson distribution

For rare events with rate \( \lambda \):

\[ \begin{equation} \mathbb{P}(X=k)=\frac{e^{-\lambda}\lambda^k}{k!} \end{equation} \]

Discrete uniform distribution

If all outcomes \(1,\dots,n\) are equally likely:

\[ \begin{equation} \mathbb{P}(X=k)=\frac{1}{n} \end{equation} \]
