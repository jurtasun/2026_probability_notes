1.3. Continuous random variables

A continuous random variable takes infinitely many possible values. Its distribution is described by a probability density function \( f(x) \), and probabilities correspond to integrals:

\[ \begin{equation} \mathbb{P}(a \le X \le b) = \int_a^b f(x)\,dx \end{equation} \]

Continuous uniform distribution

\[ \begin{equation} f(x)=\frac{1}{b-a}, \qquad a\le x\le b \end{equation} \]

Exponential distribution

\[ \begin{equation} f(x)=\lambda e^{-\lambda x}, \qquad x\ge 0 \end{equation} \]

Gaussian distribution

\[ \begin{equation} f(x)=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\sigma^2}} \exp\!\left(-\frac{(x-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right) \end{equation} \]
